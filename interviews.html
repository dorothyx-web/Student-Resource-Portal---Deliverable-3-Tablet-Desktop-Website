Tell Me About Yourself (TMAY)

Interview Introduction Guide – Help You Stand Out in Every Interview Stage

CAREER DEVELOPMENT OFFICE | University of Michigan Career Center

Start any interview strongly by highlighting your achievements and telling a concise, comprehensive story. TMAY (“Tell Me About Yourself”) is one of the most frequently asked questions in interviews, and it’s generally the first one posed. Your response should highlight your educational, technical, or business-related achievements.

It’s expected (and reasonable) to reference materials such as your resume or cover letter. However, responses should be tailored to emphasize how you meet the organization’s needs—each interview should be slightly different.

Think of this answer as an extended version of an elevator pitch and a preview of the rest of the interview. Ask yourself: What key messages do you want to convey about your ability to succeed in the role and your interest in it?

A professional job interview scene—preparing a strong TMAY helps create a positive first impression.

TMAY Key Components

Adapt your elevator pitch and personal narrative to highlight why you are the best candidate for the job/internship.

Component Detailed Content
Who are you Interviewers will likely speak with many candidates—remind them who you are:
• Restate your name
• Include a descriptive detail about your identity (e.g., your academic program or professional focus)
Pro Tip: If you go by multiple names, let the interviewer know which one you prefer.
Past and Current Work This content may have already been submitted via your resume or cover letter—you can discuss the same items. These experiences helped you secure the interview:
• Highlight key themes and common threads in your experiences
• If you share examples, keep them brief (to preview deeper discussions later)
Wrap up Conclude your TMAY response strongly and deliver a clear takeaway:
• Look to the future: How do your goals align with the position? What contributions do you hope to make?
• Identify three critical things you want the interviewer to remember about you.

Visual breakdown of the TMAY structure—follow this framework to keep your introduction organized.

Pro Tips

  • Focus on what interests the interviewer—demonstrate the qualities they seek.
  • Align your expertise and interests with the company’s needs.
  • Highlight course or internship achievements that match required skills.
  • Reflect the language of the job description to show fit.
  • Show enthusiasm for the role and organization.
  • Keep responses to around two minutes.

Program-Specific TMAY Examples with Course Projects

UX Design Track (Target Role: UX Design Intern)

"Hi, I’m Zoe Kim — I’m a junior in UMSI’s Information Science program with a focus on UX Design, and I’m excited to share how my course projects align with your intern role."

Last semester I took "Intro to UX Design" and worked on a client-based project for GLANSIS, a local environmental organization. Their website was outdated and hard for users to find conservation resources, so my task was to redesign the site’s information architecture and create high-fidelity prototypes.

I conducted 8 user interviews to identify pain points, created personas, developed wireframes, and tested with participants. The final deliverable was a Figma prototype and 10-page design brief. The GLANSIS director said they plan to use my prototype to secure redesign funding. This project taught me how to balance user needs with client goals—a skill directly relevant to your UX Design Intern role.

I’m particularly drawn to your company’s focus on accessible design. During the GLANSIS project, I ensured all text met WCAG contrast standards and added screen reader compatibility notes. I’m eager to bring this user-centric, accessible mindset to your team.

Information Analysis Track (Target Role: Business Intelligence Intern)

"Hello, I’m Raj Patel — I’m a senior in UMSI’s Information Analysis program, and I want to share how my course projects have prepared me for your Business Intelligence Intern role."

This semester I’m in "Information Analysis for Organizations," where my team worked on a project for a local small business association analyzing survey data to understand why 30% of members hadn’t renewed. I led data cleaning in SQL, created Tableau visualizations (heatmaps and dashboards), and identified “lack of networking opportunities” as a top reason.

We presented our findings to the board, and they approved a pilot program based on our recommendation. This project taught me how to translate raw data into business insights—exactly what your intern role entails.

I’m proficient in Tableau, which your job description lists as required. My dynamic dashboards let the association filter data by business type, helping me contribute immediately to your team’s analytics projects.

Data Science Track (Target Role: Data Science Intern)

"Hi, I’m Liam Scott — I’m a junior in UMSI’s Data Science specialization, and I’m excited to talk about how my course projects align with your Data Science Intern role."

In "Natural Language Processing for Health Data," I built an NLP model to classify 25,000+ tweets about medication side effects. I implemented SVM and CNN models in Python using Scikit-learn and TensorFlow. After tuning, the CNN achieved an F1 score only 0.07 below expert baseline.

This taught me to apply NLP to health data, aligning with your role’s focus on analyzing patient feedback. I used libraries your team also relies on and followed ethical AI practices by checking for demographic bias in training data.

TMAY Practice Tools & Next Steps

Crafting a strong TMAY takes practice — use the resources below to refine your narrative with course project integration.

TMAY Worksheet: Course Project Integration

  1. Introduction — “Hi, I’m [Your Name] …”
  2. Course Project 1 — “Last [Semester/Year] …”
  3. Course Project 2 — “This [Semester/Year] …”
  4. Link to the Role — “These projects have prepared me for …”
  5. Closing — “I’m excited about [Company Name] because …”
